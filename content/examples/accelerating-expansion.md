## Evidence Weight Summary

The evidence for accelerating expansion rests on an unusually strong replication structure: two independent, competing teams working with separate datasets and separate analysis pipelines reached the same unexpected conclusion within one year of each other. This form of independent replication — by teams who were in competition and had every incentive to find errors in each other's work — is considered among the most robust forms of scientific confirmation. Subsequent independent confirmation from cosmic microwave background data and baryon acoustic oscillations across multiple surveys has further consolidated the finding. The prior assumption of decelerating expansion is not a competing hypothesis — it has been replaced. The open question, treated separately in the dark energy topic, concerns the nature of what is causing the acceleration.

## Falsification Prompt

**This topic documents a completed falsification event.**

The prior hypothesis — the universe's expansion is decelerating due to gravitational attraction of matter — generated the following prediction:
- Type Ia supernovae at high redshift should appear brighter than they would in a non-decelerating universe, because the universe was expanding more slowly when the light was emitted

**This prediction was disconfirmed:**
- High-redshift supernovae appeared *fainter* than expected — the opposite of the deceleration prediction — indicating the universe was expanding *faster* when the light was emitted, not slower

**The accelerating expansion hypothesis generates the following predictions:**
- High-redshift supernovae should appear fainter than in a decelerating universe
- The expansion history of the universe as measured by independent probes — CMB, BAO — should be consistent with acceleration
- A repulsive energy component (dark energy) must be present in the universe's energy budget

**Each prediction was confirmed.**

**Pedagogical note:**  
The accelerating expansion discovery is notable for the psychological and sociological dimensions of its history. Both teams initially expected to find deceleration and treated the acceleration signal as a likely systematic error. The willingness to follow the data despite its conflict with prior expectation — and to report an unexpected result under competitive pressure — is a case study in scientific integrity under conditions of paradigm-challenging evidence.
